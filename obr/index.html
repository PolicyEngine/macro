model 02 — OBR macroeconometric model · obr-macro · UK · hosted

The OBR's published model, in Python.

An independent emulator of the OBR's published macroeconomic model — the 372-equation system behind the UK's official forecast, transpiled from the October 2025 EViews listing and solved simultaneously. Shock a tax rate or a spending line and read the deviation quarter by quarter, anchored to the November 2025 Economic and Fiscal Outlook.

Explore it live Quickstart Read the code Working paper (PDF)
PolicyEngine Macro's OBR model is an independent implementation based on the OBR's published model code and forecast data. It is not produced, maintained, or endorsed by the OBR, and its results should not be presented as official OBR estimates.
01 — what it is

An open Python implementation of the OBR's published equations.

The OBR's model is a computational representation of the UK National Accounts — first built by HM Treasury in 1970 and jointly maintained by the OBR and the Treasury since 2010. The OBR publishes its full equation listing, but as EViews source that few can run. This project transpiles that listing to Python and solves it, so the equations behind the official forecast become something you can open, shock, and inspect: 372 published equations in 17 thematic groups, over 636 variables.

The anchored baseline reproduces the published Economic and Fiscal Outlook by construction — the add-factors are set so it matches. In raw mode, with those corrections off, only some headline variables fall within calibration bands; we report that gradient openly.

This is a structural macroeconometric model in the HM Treasury / Cowles-Commission tradition. Where the OLG model derives behaviour from optimising households and firms, this one reproduces the estimated relationships and identities the official forecaster relies on: 29 equations econometrically estimated, 216 identities, 138 calibrated or technical relationships, and 253 exogenous inputs — the judgements and assumptions you get to change.

02 — the live explorer

The interactive documentation.

The model ships with a live dashboard — the fastest way to understand it. 03 — quickstart

Shock a variable, read the deviation.

The package installs straight from GitHub — the OBR equation listing, the November 2025 EFO forecast tables, and the ONS input snapshot ship inside the package. A reform is one call: pick an exogenous variable, apply a shock (levels are £m per quarter), read the deviation from baseline. 

pip install git+https://github.com/PolicyEngine/obr-macroeconomic-model
from obr_macro import run_reform

# £5bn/yr government spending shock (£1.25bn per quarter)
results = run_reform(
    name="Fiscal Stimulus",
    var="CGG",        # government consumption (exogenous)
    shock=1250,        # £1.25bn per quarter, in £m
    periods=12,
)
print(results[["period", "delta_gdp_bn", "pct_gdp"]])
# ΔGDP ≈ £1.25bn in the impact quarter, near-flat thereafter —
# under the demand closure the impact multiplier is ~1 by construction

run the five bundled scenarios with charts: python -m obr_macro.reform_analysis — a £5bn spending rise, 5pp corporation-tax cut and rise, £10bn public investment (£3bn nominal CGIPS per quarter), and a £10bn austerity cut. Charts land in outputs/.

04 — how it works

Transpile, solve, swap the closure.

The engine turns a static equation listing into a live policy tool in five stages:

stagewhat it does
Transpile Rewrites every EViews line — dlog(), @elem, lags like X(-1), error-correction terms — into an equivalent Python expression.
Initialise Loads history from the OBR's detailed forecast tables; any variable a published equation needs but the data doesn't supply is created and seeded.
Gauss–Seidel solve The 372 equations depend on each other, so each quarter is solved simultaneously: cycle through the equations, update values, repeat until nothing moves.
Closure swap The GDP identity is switched off in the OBR file, so for a shock GDPM is swapped in and inventories (DINV) become the residual — output responds to the policy instead of being fixed.
Shock & compare Solves the shocked and baseline paths with identical model structure and reports the difference, isolating the policy effect from the underlying forecast.
Of the 261 endogenous variables the published listing referenced but the OBR's outlook did not print, 199 had ONS series codes — pulling those observed series (a 348-series ONS snapshot, vendored with the package) gave the circular fiscal and financial sub-blocks real balancing data and stopped them diverging.
05 — the levers

253 exogenous inputs, 383 solved variables.

Every variable is in one of two camps — the test is whether it has its own equation. Reforms enter through the 253 exogenous inputs (fiscal levers, market assumptions, world demand, demographics); the results come out of the 383 the model solves. The most useful:

06 — forecasting

The OBR's own method: held add-factors.

Beyond one-off shocks, the emulator forecasts the way the OBR does: compute the residuals (add-factors) that reconcile each behavioural equation to recent data over a base window of 2024Q1–2025Q4, hold them constant, and project eight quarters forward to 2027Q4. Exogenous inputs are held at their last value, and the frozen corrections anchor the model's structural dynamics — a genuine forecast, not a replay of the input. Scenario solves run twelve quarters, 2025Q1–2027Q4.

07 — calibration

Held-add-factor forecast vs the OBR.

Scored against the OBR's published outlook over the projected window, the core real block tracks closely; the incomes and profit blocks are harder. Of 16 headline variables, 10 are actually computed by the model, and 6 of those land within band.

real GDP 2.2% MAPE within band
consumption 3.6% MAPE within band
unemployment 0.75pp within band
current account 0.9% of GDP within band

Held-add-factor forecast, base 2024Q1–2025Q4 projected to 2027Q4, versus the OBR. "Within band" collapses per-metric thresholds — rates within 1.0pp, net balances within 1.5% of GDP, levels within 10% MAPE. The trade balance also sits within band (0.11% of GDP); business investment (17.9%), company profits (47.4%) and household income (~12%) sit over band. Two further honesty caveats from the repository's own scorecards: the forecast is initialised at the EFO values it is scored against, which flatters the fit; and with the add-factors switched off entirely, the raw de-seeded model tracks the OBR poorly — the add-factors are doing real work, exactly as they do in the OBR's own process.

08 — caveats

An emulator, not the OBR.

Results depend entirely on your assumptions — they are not OBR forecasts. This is an independent Python re-implementation, and its limits are documented:

caveatdetail
Approximated add-factors Recent corrections are averaged and held flat over the forecast. The OBR's judgemental, quarter-by-quarter add-factors are not reproduced.
Impact multiplier ~1 by construction Under the demand closure a spending shock lands directly in the GDP identity and the behavioural second round is largely inactive — the OBR's published impact multiplier for current spending is ~0.45.
Corporation-tax channel unstable The investment closure activates the OBR's published TCPRO → cost-of-capital → investment chain, but the re-solved path does not converge here; corp-tax scenarios are excluded from the dashboard explorer until recalibrated. The business-investment equation is itself reconstructed from the OBR's commented, truncated published line.
Passthrough channels Exports, imports and CPI are exogenous in this setup, held at the OBR value — dead channels score 0.00% error without being behavioural wins.
No behavioural micro Aggregate equations only — no household- or firm-level behavioural responses. Pair with PolicyEngine's microsimulation for distributional analysis.
Data vintage Current-vintage ONS series merged into an autumn-2025 EFO baseline; where the ONS has revised history the identities don't close exactly, and that slack lands in the add-factors.
