01 — why macro

A policy score is only the first step.

Microsimulation shows who pays and who benefits immediately. Macro models trace what happens next as work, saving, investment, wages, prices, and interest rates respond. PolicyEngine Macro makes both layers available through open models and a shared interface.

These models are not interchangeable. Start with the decision you need to make, then inspect the model's evidence and limitations before using its result.

obr-macroUKhosted

OBR macroeconometric model

How does a UK tax change move growth and the deficit over 3–5 years? A Python emulator of the OBR's own 372-equation forecasting model, anchored to the November 2025 Economic and Fiscal Outlook. (A March 2026 EFO has since been published; we have not re-anchored to it yet.)

boe-svarUKhosted

Bank of England structural VAR model

What is driving UK GDP and inflation right now? A Python replication of the Bank of England's structural VAR — eight quarterly variables, six identified shocks. Forecasts with credible bands and explains forecast revisions. Does not score reforms.

read the model →
frb-usUShosted

FRB/US

What does a 100bp Fed tightening do to US GDP, unemployment and inflation? A from-scratch Python implementation of the Fed's large-scale macroeconometric model, from the Board's public model definition and April 2026 LONGBASE data. Raw variable shocks only — it does not score PolicyEngine reforms. VAR expectations only; model-consistent expectations not yet implemented.

read the model →
psl-ogUKUSlocal

PSL overlapping generations model

What does a reform do to work, saving and the capital stock over decades? A dynamic general-equilibrium model of 80 age cohorts on OG-Core, with tax functions estimated from PolicyEngine microdata. Solves the long-run steady state and the transition path. Slow: 17+ minutes per solve.

read the model →
pe-microsimUKUShosted

PolicyEngine tax-benefit microsimulation

What does this reform mean for one specific family? PolicyEngine's UK and US tax-benefit microsimulation — the same engine behind policyengine.org. Computes taxes, benefits and net income under current law or a reform, and scores reforms across the population. Its ground truth is statute itself.

04 — validation

Validation is a gradient, not a badge.

Most model suites publish a badge. We publish the distance to the thing we are answerable to — and where there is nothing to be answerable to, we say so instead of manufacturing a number. The five models fall into three classes, and the class matters more than any single percentage.

The verification gradient, model by model
ModelVerification classHeadline result
obr-macroReplication with a published anchorTracks the November 2025 EFO to a GDP MAPE of 0.18%
boe-svarReplication with a published anchorOut-of-sample RMSE 0.32pp, 14 of 14 outturns inside the 68% bands
frb-usReplication with a published anchorMatches the Fed's own pyfrbus 1.0.0 to 6.0×10⁻⁹ on shock responses
pe-microsimValidated against statuteExact household arithmetic — not an approximation
psl-ogCalibrated counterfactual — no ground truthTargets met by construction; no published replication exists

The distinction that does the work: a match on a quantity that was imposed or targeted is anchoring, not validation. The OBR emulator's 0.18% is a by-construction invariant — the same equations miss by 5.70% free-running, and we publish that number too. Two of its equations are also known to be numerically inert for want of a source series, and every published OBR figure is computed that way. Every model's numbers beside the official ones →
