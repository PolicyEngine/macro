macromod is a suite of macroeconomic simulation models for scoring public policy — general-equilibrium dynamics for GDP, investment, consumption, revenue, and debt, driven by the same tax-and-benefit rules and microdata that power PolicyEngine, and improved in the open.

01 — the idea

From static scoring to the full economy.

Microsimulation tells you who pays what the morning after a reform. But people respond: they work, save, and invest differently, firms adjust capital, wages and interest rates move, and the revenue estimate moves with them. Most published policy scores stop before that second act — or bolt it on with a single hand-picked elasticity.

macromod makes the second act a model, not a fudge factor. Each model in the suite is a structural, general-equilibrium engine that takes a policy reform — expressed in PolicyEngine's rules engine, at full statutory detail — and solves for the economy's response: changes in macroeconomic aggregates (GDP, investment, consumption), wages, interest rates, and tax revenues, from the first year of transition to the new long-run steady state.

02 — the models

One suite, many lenses on the same economy.

Different questions need different macro machinery. Every model in the suite scores the same PolicyEngine reform objects and reports the same real-world quantities, so results are comparable across model classes — and every model gets its own page: a 30-second explainer, the method, and the calibration evidence.

olgshipped

Overlapping generations

A dynamic general-equilibrium model of 80 age cohorts working, saving, and retiring — built on OG-Core, calibrated to national accounts, with tax functions estimated from PolicyEngine microdata. Solves the long-run steady state and the year-by-year transition path for GDP, investment, consumption, revenue, and debt.

obrshipped

OBR macroeconometric model

A Python emulator of the OBR's own published forecasting model — 372 structural equations solved simultaneously by Gauss–Seidel iteration. Runs the fiscal-multiplier and tax-shock experiments the OBR uses to score Budget measures, quarter by quarter, anchored to the official Economic and Fiscal Outlook.

03 — the pipeline

Statute in, economy out.

A reform enters as a PolicyEngine policy object — actual parameters of the tax and benefit system, not a stylized wedge. PolicyEngine's UK microsimulation — built on the Family Resources Survey, HMRC income data, and household expenditure and wealth surveys — computes effective and marginal tax rates across the population under baseline and reform, and the macro model fits tax functions to that microdata. So it sees the reform's true shape across the income and age distribution. The calibration is anchored to ONS, OBR, and Bank of England data, then the model solves for the general-equilibrium response.

The same interface scores structural shocks too — corporation tax, productivity, government spending — so fiscal packages that mix statutory and structural changes score in one run.

04 — outputs

Real-world quantities, not model units.

Every run maps back to current-price national-accounts aggregates. A steady-state solve answers the long-run question; a transition path gives the fiscal-year-by-fiscal-year profile a budget process needs.
