Open models for
the economy.

PolicyEngine Macro is a suite of five open economic models: an OBR emulator and a Bank of England structural VAR for the UK, the Fed's FRB/US model in Python for the US, and the PSL overlapping-generations model and PolicyEngine microsimulation for both countries. Each answers a different class of question — near-term fiscal scenarios, shock decomposition, long-run structural change, household tax and benefit effects — and each is checked against a published counterpart, against statute, or against calibration targets, with the difference stated rather than blurred.

01 — the idea

From static scores to microfounded models.

Policy analysis usually stops at a static score — who pays what the morning after a reform. But people respond: work, saving, and investment shift; wages, prices, and interest rates move, and the fiscal arithmetic moves with them. Modeling that response has been the preserve of institutions with in-house model teams.

PolicyEngine Macro opens those engines up: Python implementations of the OBR's and the Fed's official equation systems for the near term, a Bayesian structural VAR that reads the economy in shock terms, an overlapping-generations model for the long run, and a tax-benefit microsimulation for the household detail underneath.

We publish what each one is worth. Validation is a gradient, not a badge. Three models are replications with published anchors, and we report the gap: the OBR emulator tracks the November 2025 EFO with a GDP MAPE of 0.18%, the structural VAR posts an out-of-sample RMSE of 0.32pp with 14 of 14 outturns inside its 68% bands, and FRB/US matches the Fed's own pyfrbus 1.0.0 to 6.0×10⁻⁹ on shock responses. The microsimulation is validated against statute. The overlapping-generations model has no published ground truth — it is a calibrated counterfactual, and we say so rather than dress it as a replication. See the numbers beside the official ones →

02 — the models

One suite, many lenses on the economy.

Three are hosted; FRB/US and the overlapping-generations model run locally, the latter taking upward of 17 minutes per solve. Each keeps its own assumptions, inputs, and outputs — no single reform object flows through all five. The four macro models each have a working paper, 28 to 36 pages, linked from the model's own page.

psl-ogUKUSlocal only

PSL overlapping generations model

A dynamic general-equilibrium model of 80 age cohorts working, saving, and retiring — built on OG-Core, with tax functions estimated from PolicyEngine microdata. Solves the long-run steady state and the year-by-year transition path for GDP, investment, consumption, revenue, and debt. Calibrated to targets rather than replicated against a published model — there is none to replicate.

read the model →
obr-macroUKhosted

OBR macroeconometric model

A Python emulator of the OBR's own published forecasting model — 372 structural equations solved simultaneously by Gauss–Seidel iteration. Runs fiscal-multiplier and tax-shock experiments quarter by quarter, anchored to the November 2025 Economic and Fiscal Outlook. (A March 2026 EFO has since been published; we have not re-anchored to it yet.)

read the model →
boe-svarUKhosted

Bank of England structural VAR model

A Python replication of the Bank of England's structural VAR for the UK — eight quarterly variables, six identified shocks. Decomposes GDP and inflation into their drivers, forecasts with credible bands, and explains forecast revisions.

read the model →
pe-microsimUKUShosted

PolicyEngine tax-benefit microsimulation

PolicyEngine's UK and US tax-benefit microsimulation — the same engine behind policyengine.org. Computes a household's taxes, benefits, and net income under current law or a reform, and scores reforms across the population. Its ground truth is statute itself.

read the model →
frb-usUSlocal

FRB/US

A from-scratch Python implementation of the Fed's large-scale US macroeconometric model, built from the public model definition and April 2026 LONGBASE data. Reproduces LONGBASE to 5.6×10⁻¹⁷ and matches pyfrbus 1.0.0 shock responses to 6.0×10⁻⁹; model-consistent expectations not yet implemented.

03 — the workflow

Different questions need different models.

Start with a question and choose the lens: the OBR emulator for near-term UK fiscal scenarios, the structural VAR for forecasts and what's driving the data, FRB/US for US macro dynamics, the overlapping-generations model for long-run structural change, and PolicyEngine for household and distributional effects.

Every analysis records its assumptions, data vintage, model version, and units, and each result is a deviation from an official baseline in real-world units: £ per year for a household, £bn and % of GDP for the economy. One CLI, one Python API, and a hosted MCP server with ten tools reach the models — including score_reform, which costs a reform through the OBR static bridge. Connect a client →

Built in the open. Read it, run it, contribute.

Every model, dataset decision, and validation report lives in a public repository, and the validation page states the anchor and vintage behind every figure. File an issue, suggest the next model, or open a pull request.
