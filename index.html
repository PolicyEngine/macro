Open models for
the economy.

PolicyEngine Macro helps researchers, analysts, and developers answer economic and public-policy questions with transparent, reproducible models — analyse reforms, simulate shocks, forecast GDP and inflation, investigate what's driving the economy, and study long-run structural change. The suite spans the UK — an OBR emulator and a Bank of England structural VAR — and the US, with a validated Python implementation of the Fed's FRB/US model; the PSL overlapping-generations model and the PolicyEngine microsimulation cover both countries. One suite, under the PolicyEngine umbrella. Each model answers a different class of question — it does not promise one comparable number across all of them.

01 — the idea

From static scores to microfounded models.

Policy analysis usually stops at a static score — who pays what the morning after a reform. But people respond: they work, save, and invest differently; firms adjust; wages, prices, and interest rates move, and the fiscal arithmetic moves with them. Modeling that response has been the preserve of institutions with in-house model teams.

PolicyEngine Macro opens it up — the full economy's answer, a model away: an overlapping-generations engine for the long run, an independent Python implementation of the OBR's published equations for the near-term fiscal path, and a Bayesian structural VAR that reads the economy in shock terms at the latest available data vintage. Every engine open source, every result reproducible, one command away.

02 — the models

One suite, many lenses on the economy.

Different questions need different macro machinery. Each model answers a different class of economic question — near-term fiscal scenarios, forecasts and shock identification, long-run structural change, or household-level tax and benefit effects. PolicyEngine Macro gives you a common way to discover, run, and interpret them, while preserving the assumptions and outputs specific to each model. Every model gets its own page: a 30-second explainer, the method, and the calibration evidence.

psl-ogUKUSlocal onlyexperimental

PSL overlapping generations model

A dynamic general-equilibrium model of 80 age cohorts working, saving, and retiring — built on OG-Core, calibrated to national accounts, with tax functions estimated from PolicyEngine microdata. Solves the long-run steady state and the year-by-year transition path for GDP, investment, consumption, revenue, and debt.

obr-macroUKhosted

OBR macroeconometric model

A Python emulator of the OBR's own published forecasting model — 372 structural equations solved simultaneously by Gauss–Seidel iteration. Runs fiscal-multiplier and tax-shock experiments quarter by quarter, anchored to the official Economic and Fiscal Outlook.

boe-svarUKhosted

Bank of England structural VAR model

A Python replication of the Bank of England's structural VAR for the UK economy — eight quarterly variables, six identified shocks (world demand, energy, and supply; UK demand, supply, and monetary policy). Decomposes GDP and inflation into their drivers, forecasts with credible bands, and explains forecast revisions.

pe-microsimUKUShosted

PolicyEngine tax-benefit microsimulation

The micro member of the suite: PolicyEngine's tax-benefit microsimulation package for the UK and US — the same engine behind policyengine.org. Computes a specific family's taxes, benefits, and net income under current law or a reform, at person and household resolution; population-level reform scoring runs hosted and locally.

read the model →
frb-usUSlocal

FRB/US

A from-scratch Python implementation of the Federal Reserve Board's large-scale US macroeconometric model, built from the Fed's public model definition and April 2026 LONGBASE data. VAR-expectations solver validated against the Fed's own pyfrbus to ~1e-9; baseline tracking reproduces LONGBASE to machine precision. Model-consistent expectations are not yet implemented.

03 — the workflow

Different questions need different models.

Start with a question — a reform, a shock, a forecast, or a structural change. PolicyEngine Macro helps you choose the appropriate lens: the OBR emulator for near-term fiscal scenarios, the structural VAR for forecasts and what's driving the data, the overlapping-generations model for long-run structural change, and PolicyEngine for household and distributional effects. Each runs at its own resolution, on its own inputs — no single reform object flows through all four.

Every analysis records its assumptions, data vintage, model version, and units, and each result is a deviation from an official baseline — ONS, OBR, and Bank of England data — in real-world units: £ per year for a household, £bn and % of GDP for the economy. One CLI, one MCP server, and one Python API reach every model, and results are labelled as a forecast, a simulation, or a historical estimate.

Built in the open. Read it, run it, contribute.

Every model, dataset decision, and validation report lives in a public repository — nothing behind a login, nothing you can't rerun. File an issue, suggest the next model, or open a pull request.
