Match the model to the question — and the country.

The first cut is geographic: obr-macro and boe-svar are UK models, frb-us is a US model, and psl-og and pe-microsim span both countries. The second cut is the question:

  • "How does this tax change affect UK growth and the deficit over the next 3–5 years?" — the OBR model. Near-term fiscal scoring in the language of the official forecast.
  • "What does a 100bp Fed tightening — or a US fiscal shock — do to GDP, unemployment, and inflation?" — FRB/US, via frbus_shock on the hosted server or the CLI. Shocks to model variables, not PolicyEngine reforms.
  • "What does this reform do to work incentives, saving, and the long-run size of the economy?" — the OLG model, which captures how households of every age re-optimise over their lifetimes.
  • "What's driving UK GDP and inflation — and why did the forecast change?" — the structural VAR. It decomposes the latest data vintage (currently 2024 Q2) into named structural shocks, forecasts with credible bands, and splits a revision into news and reassessment. It does not score reforms.
  • "What does this reform mean for a nurse on £35,000 with two kids?" — PolicyEngine. It runs the actual UK or US statute for that exact family, before and after.
  • "I want both the transition and the destination." — run both UK scoring models (OBR and OLG). Agreement raises confidence; divergence marks where long-run behavioural response departs from near-term empirical dynamics.

Verification gradient. The third cut is how far a model can be checked against ground truth at all — weight its answers accordingly. obr-macro, boe-svar and frb-us are replications: each reproduces a specific published artefact, so a deviation from it is a meaningful number. pe-microsim is validated against statute — checkable rule by rule, with no forecast error to speak of. psl-og is a structural counterfactual: calibrated to national-accounts targets, with no published result to validate against. A replication you can audit against its anchor; statute you can audit against the law; a calibrated counterfactual you can only reason about — and no amount of agreement with a target it was fitted to changes that. reference

Each model has its own reference page.

obr-macro · UK · hosted

OBR macroeconometric model

The OBR publishes its equation listing as EViews source; this transpiles those 372 equations to Python and solves them, 636 variables over 17 thematic groups, with 253 exogenous inputs as the levers. Add-factors anchor the baseline to the November 2025 EFO — the March 2026 vintage has not been adopted. Two household-income equations are numerically inert, their inputs MAJGDP and CORP being absent from the databank, and every published figure is computed that way.

read the OBR reference → boe-svar · UK · hosted

Bank of England structural VAR model

Brignone & Piffer (2025), BoE Macro Technical Paper No. 3, shipped without a replication package; this rebuilds the pipeline end to end. Eight quarterly series, three global and five UK, estimated 1992Q1–2023Q2 under a Minnesota prior, with zero and sign restrictions naming six shocks. The frozen 2024Q2 edge is what makes its forecast scoreable out of sample.

read the SVAR reference → frb-us · US · hosted

FRB/US

Parses the Board's public model.xml rather than re-specifying it, and solves all 284 equations per quarter by damped Newton on an analytic sparse Jacobian, against April 2026 LONGBASE. Shocks are additions to an equation's error term, read under a selectable policy rule. Only the VAR-expectations path exists; mcontrol, model-consistent expectations and stochastic simulation do not.

read the FRB/US reference → psl-og · UK · US · local

PSL overlapping generations model

OG-Core, deployed through its OG-UK calibration: households aged 20 to 99 choosing labour supply and saving over their remaining lifetimes, with Gouveia–Strauss tax functions fitted to PolicyEngine microdata so the reform's shape across income and age is what the model sees. Solves a steady state or a 60-year transition path. The UK integration is wired; OG-USA is newer.

read the OLG reference → pe-microsim · UK · US · hosted

PolicyEngine tax-benefit microsimulation

Four layers: a simulation framework forked from OpenFisca, a statutory rule package per country, and the analyst-facing policyengine package on top, which pins the model and dataset versions each release certifies. Reforms are a dict of parameter path to new value, identical in shape at household and population level. Population runs use enhanced_frs_2023_24 (53,000 households) and CPS microdata; household calculations need no data access at all.

papers

Each macro model ships with a paper.

Readable in the browser or as PDF. LaTeX sources and the scripts that regenerate every figure live in papers/, so each number on a chart is traceable to a committed artefact. pe-microsim is the exception with no paper: its rules are documented as statute at policyengine.org.

obr-macro · 35 pages · read in browser

An open emulator of the OBR macroeconometric model

Transpiles the OBR's 372 published EViews equations into Python, solves them by Gauss–Seidel iteration, and anchors the baseline to the November 2025 Economic and Fiscal Outlook.

Anchored to the Nov 2025 EFO: GDP MAPE 0.18%, consumption 0.29% over 2025Q1–2027Q4 — hard-gated in CI. Free-running fit reported without flattery: only 4 of 11 computed variables in band. read the OBR working paper → boe-svar · 28 pages · read in browser

Replicating the Bank of England's structural VAR

An eight-variable Bayesian SVAR with a Minnesota prior and zero plus sign restrictions imposed via Arias et al. (2018), identifying six named structural shocks.

Out-of-sample from the 2024Q2 edge: RMSE 0.32pp for both GDP growth and CPI inflation, with 14/14 outturns inside the 68% bands. Global shocks explain 40.9% of GDP and 50.1% of CPI FEV at one year, vs ~40% and ~50% in the paper. read the SVAR working paper → frb-us · 36 pages · read in browser

A modern Python implementation of FRB/US

A from-scratch reimplementation of the Fed's 284-equation FRB/US model under VAR expectations, with a sparse analytic Jacobian and a damped Newton solver.

Baseline reproduces LONGBASE to 5.6e-17 (machine precision); an identical 100bp funds-rate shock matches the Fed's pyfrbus to 1.4e-8 against current pyfrbus 1.1.1 (6.0e-9 vs 1.0.0) across all 284 variables and 20 quarters. read the FRB/US working paper → psl-og · 34 pages · read in browser

The PSL overlapping-generations model in PolicyEngine Macro

OG-Core deployed through its OG-UK calibration, with 80 age cohorts and effective- and marginal-tax-rate functions estimated from PolicyEngine microdata.

No published ground truth — unlike the OBR, SVAR and FRB/US members this is a structural counterfactual, calibrated to ONS and OBR national-accounts targets rather than validated against a replication anchor. read the OLG working paper →
shared pipeline

What the models have in common.

For the scoring engines the shape of the work is the same: build a reform, solve baseline and reform, read the difference in real-world quantities. All report deviations from a baseline anchored to official data — always "relative to current forecast", never a raw level to be misread. The structural VAR sits upstream, establishing the empirical starting point; PolicyEngine sits underneath, supplying the reform objects and the household-level answer.
