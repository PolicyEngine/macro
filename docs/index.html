Five models. Two economies, read five ways.

Each model answers a different class of question — near-term fiscal scenarios, forecasts and shock identification, US macro dynamics, long-run structural change, and household or distributional effects. Two models are UK-specific, one is US-specific, and two span both countries. This is the reference: what each model is, how they differ, and when to reach for which.

01 — the models

Each model has its own reference page.

The OLG and OBR models score policy changes into macroeconomic aggregates. The structural VAR is the UK empirical member: it reads and forecasts the economy rather than scoring reforms. FRB/US is the US macroeconometric member, validated against the Fed's published baseline and solver. PolicyEngine is the micro member, answering at person and household resolution. Start with the model that matches your question, or read on for the comparison.

obr-macro · UK · hosted

OBR macroeconometric model

A Python emulator of the OBR's own 372-equation forecasting model. Best for near-term fiscal multipliers scored the way the official forecaster scores them, quarter by quarter. Anchored mode reproduces the November 2025 EFO.

read the OBR reference → boe-svar · UK · hosted

Bank of England structural VAR model

A Python replication of the Bank of England's 8-variable Bayesian SVAR. Best for reading the current state of the economy in structural-shock terms, short-run fan-chart forecasts, and forecast-revision narratives. Does not score reforms.

read the SVAR reference → frb-us · US · local

FRB/US

A from-scratch Python implementation of the Fed's FRB/US model, built from the Fed's public model definition (284 VAR-expectations equations) and April 2026 LONGBASE data. Best for US macro dynamics — monetary and fiscal shocks under VAR expectations.

read the code → psl-og · UK · US · local

PSL overlapping generations model

A structural general-equilibrium model of 80 age cohorts on OG-Core, with tax functions from PolicyEngine microdata. Best for long-run, behaviour-driven questions: how a tax change reshapes work, saving, and the capital stock over decades. The UK integration (OG-UK) is wired today; the US side (OG-USA) is newer.

read the OLG reference → pe-microsim · UK · US · hosted

PolicyEngine tax-benefit microsimulation

PolicyEngine's UK & US tax-benefit engine — the same one behind policyengine.org. Best for household-resolution questions: what a specific family pays and receives under current law or a reform, and population-level scoring on a 53,000-household enhanced FRS for the UK.

02 — the comparison

Five model classes, side by side.

The models are complementary, not competing. Two reproduce official forecasters' systems — the OBR's for the UK, the Fed's for the US; one imposes minimal theory and reads what is driving the UK economy; one derives behaviour from first principles for the long run; and one drops from aggregates to people, computing the actual statute for a specific household.

03 — which to use

Match the model to the question — and the country.

The first cut is geographic: obr-macro and boe-svar are UK models, frb-us is a US model, and psl-og and pe-microsim span both countries. The second cut is the question:

  • "How does this tax change affect UK growth and the deficit over the next 3–5 years?" — the OBR model. Near-term fiscal scoring in the language of the official forecast.
  • "What does a 100bp Fed tightening — or a US fiscal shock — do to GDP, unemployment, and inflation?" — FRB/US, locally. (VAR expectations only; model-consistent expectations, mcontrol, and stochastic simulations are not yet implemented.)
  • "What does this reform do to work incentives, saving, and the long-run size of the economy?" — the OLG model, which captures how households of every age re-optimise over their lifetimes.
  • "What's driving UK GDP and inflation — and why did the forecast change?" — the structural VAR. It decomposes the latest data vintage (currently 2024 Q2) into named structural shocks, forecasts with credible bands, and splits a revision into news and reassessment. It does not score reforms.
  • "What does this reform mean for a nurse on £35,000 with two kids?" — PolicyEngine. It runs the actual UK or US statute for that exact family, before and after.
  • "I want both the transition and the destination." — run both UK scoring models (OBR and OLG) and read the near-term multiplier against the long-run equilibrium.

Verification gradient. The models differ in how far they can be checked against ground truth — weight their answers accordingly. Three are replications with published anchors: the OBR emulator reproduces the November 2025 EFO in anchored mode (GDP MAPE 0.18%, consumption 0.29%, hard-gated in nightly CI; its free-running fit is report-only, and weak); the structural VAR matches the Bank of England paper's figures, with FEVD shares inside the paper's bands; FRB/US reproduces the Fed's LONGBASE database to machine precision and matches pyfrbus shock responses to ~1e-9. pe-microsim is validated against statute itself, checkable rule by rule. psl-og is a structural counterfactual: calibrated to national-accounts targets, with no published result to validate against. A replication you can audit against its anchor; statute you can audit against the law; a calibrated counterfactual you can only reason about.

Running the same reform through both UK scoring models is the point of a suite: agreement raises confidence, and divergence usually marks where long-run behavioural response departs from near-term empirical dynamics.
04 — the shared pipeline

What the models have in common.

For the scoring engines the shape of the work is the same: build a reform, solve baseline and reform, read the difference in real-world quantities. The OLG model expresses the reform as statute translated into tax functions; the OBR model and FRB/US express it as shocks to their equation systems. All report deviations from a baseline anchored to official data — always "relative to current forecast", never a raw level to be misread. The structural VAR sits upstream, establishing the empirical starting point; PolicyEngine sits underneath, supplying the reform objects and the household-level answer.
