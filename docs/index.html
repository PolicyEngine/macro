Five models. Two economies, read five ways.

PolicyEngine Macro is a suite where each model answers a different class of economic question — near-term fiscal scenarios, forecasts and shock identification, US macro dynamics under VAR expectations, long-run structural change, and household or distributional effects. Two models are UK-specific, one is US-specific, and two span both countries. It is not one reform scored five ways: different questions call for different machinery. This is the reference: what each model is, how they differ, and when to reach for which.

01 — the models

Each model has its own reference page.

The OLG and OBR models take the same kind of input — a policy change — and report the same kind of output — its effect on macroeconomic aggregates. The structural VAR is the UK empirical member: it reads the current state of the economy and forecasts it, rather than scoring reforms. FRB/US is the US macroeconometric member: the Fed's own large-scale model, reimplemented in Python and validated against the Fed's published baseline and solver. PolicyEngine is the micro member: it answers at person and household resolution rather than in aggregates. Start with the model that matches your question, or read on for the comparison.

obr-macro · UK · hosted

OBR macroeconometric model

A Python emulator of the OBR's own 372-equation forecasting model. Best for near-term fiscal multipliers scored the way the official forecaster scores them, quarter by quarter. Anchored mode reproduces the November 2025 EFO.

read the OBR reference → boe-svar · UK · hosted

Bank of England structural VAR model

A Python replication of the Bank of England's 8-variable Bayesian SVAR. Best for reading the current state of the economy in structural-shock terms, short-run fan-chart forecasts, and forecast-revision narratives. Does not score reforms.

read the SVAR reference → frb-us · US · local

FRB/US

A from-scratch Python implementation of the Fed's FRB/US model, built from the Fed's public model definition (284 VAR-expectations equations) and April 2026 LONGBASE data. Best for US macro dynamics — monetary and fiscal shocks under VAR expectations.

read the code → psl-og · UK · US · local

PSL overlapping generations model

A structural general-equilibrium model of 80 age cohorts on OG-Core, with tax functions from PolicyEngine microdata. Best for long-run, behaviour-driven questions: how a tax change reshapes work, saving, and the capital stock over decades. The UK integration (OG-UK) is wired today; the US side (OG-USA) is newer.

read the OLG reference → pe-microsim · UK · US · hosted

PolicyEngine tax-benefit microsimulation

PolicyEngine's UK & US tax-benefit engine — the same one behind policyengine.org. Best for household-resolution questions: what a specific family pays and receives under current law or a reform, and population-level scoring on a 53,000-household enhanced FRS for the UK.

02 — the comparison

Five model classes, side by side.

The models are complementary, not competing. Two reproduce official forecasters' empirical systems — the OBR's for the UK, the Fed's for the US — and answer what happens over the next few years; one imposes minimal theory and answers what is driving the UK economy at the latest available data vintage (currently 2024 Q2); one derives behaviour from first principles and answers where the economy settles in the long run; and one drops from aggregates to people, computing the actual statute for a specific household.

03 — which to use

Match the model to the question — and the country.

The first cut is geographic: obr-macro and boe-svar are UK models, frb-us is a US model, and psl-og and pe-microsim span both countries. The second cut is the question:

  • "How does this tax change affect UK growth and the deficit over the next 3–5 years?" — the OBR model. It's built for near-term fiscal scoring and speaks the same language as the official forecast.
  • "What does a 100bp Fed tightening — or a US fiscal shock — do to GDP, unemployment, and inflation?" — FRB/US. It runs the Fed's own model equations under VAR expectations, locally. (Model-consistent expectations, mcontrol, and stochastic simulations are not yet implemented.)
  • "What does this reform do to work incentives, saving, and the long-run size of the economy?" — the OLG model, for the UK today and, more recently, the US. It captures how households of every age re-optimise over their lifetimes.
  • "What's driving UK GDP and inflation at the latest data vintage — and why did the forecast change since the quarter before?" — the structural VAR. It decomposes the data at the latest available vintage (currently 2024 Q2) into named structural shocks, forecasts with credible bands, and splits a forecast revision into news and reassessment. It does not score reforms.
  • "What does this reform mean for a nurse on £35,000 with two kids?" — PolicyEngine. It runs the actual UK or US statute for that exact family and reads off taxes, benefits, and net income, before and after.
  • "I want both the transition and the destination." — run both UK scoring models (OBR and OLG). They report comparable real-world aggregates, so you can read the near-term multiplier against the long-run equilibrium.

Verification gradient. The models also differ in how far they can be checked against ground truth, and that should temper how much weight you put on each answer. Three of the five are replications with published anchors. The OBR emulator is validated against the OBR's Economic and Fiscal Outlook tables: in anchored mode it reproduces the November 2025 EFO with a GDP MAPE of 0.18% and consumption 0.29%, hard-gated in nightly CI; its free-running fit is deliberately report-only, and weak. The structural VAR is validated against the Bank of England paper's figures — sign and zero restrictions hold on every accepted draw, and FEVD shares fall within the paper's bands. FRB/US is validated against the Fed's own artefacts: baseline tracking reproduces the LONGBASE database to machine precision (5.6e-17), and a 100bp funds-rate shock matches the Fed's pyfrbus solver to ~1e-9 (a +1pp tightening puts the GDP trough at −0.55% and unemployment +0.26pp). psl-og is a structural counterfactual model with no ground truth: it is calibrated to national-accounts targets, not validated against a published result — and its US wiring is newer. pe-microsim is validated against statute itself, checkable rule by rule against the tax and benefit code. A replication you can audit against its anchor; statute you can audit against the law; a calibrated counterfactual you can only reason about.

Running the same reform through both UK scoring models is the point of a suite: agreement raises confidence, and a divergence is informative — it usually marks where long-run behavioural response departs from near-term empirical dynamics.
04 — the shared pipeline

What the models have in common.

For the scoring engines, the shape of the work is the same: build a reform, solve baseline and reform, read the difference in real-world quantities — GDP, consumption, investment, revenue. The OLG model expresses the reform as PolicyEngine statute translated into tax functions; the OBR model expresses it as a shock to an exogenous variable; FRB/US expresses it as a shock experiment on the Fed's equations. All report the deviation from a baseline anchored to official data, so a result is always "relative to current forecast", never a raw level to be misread. The structural VAR sits upstream of that loop: it establishes the empirical starting point — which shocks the UK economy is currently absorbing — from the same public official data. And PolicyEngine sits underneath: it supplies the reform objects the suite scores and the household-level answer to the same question the macro engines answer in aggregates.
