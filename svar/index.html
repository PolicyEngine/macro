model 03 — Bank of England structural VAR · boe-svar · UK · hosted

Read the economy in shocks, not headlines.

A Python replication of the Bank of England's structural VAR for the UK economy — an 8-variable Bayesian time-series model that decomposes UK GDP and inflation into six structural shocks, forecasts with credible bands, and explains why a forecast changed between quarters.

Quickstart Read the code Working paper (PDF)
01 — what it is

The Bank's own empirical lens, replicated in the open.

Data vintage. The packaged input series currently end in 2024 Q2. Every forecast and decomposition on this page is stated relative to that vintage and its forecast origin — not the present quarter.
Independent replication. PolicyEngine Macro's structural VAR is an independent replication of the Bank of England's published structural VAR for the UK economy (Brignone & Piffer, 2025; BoE Macro Technical Paper No. 3). It is not produced, maintained, or endorsed by the Bank of England.

In July 2025 the Bank of England published Macro Technical Paper No. 3 — A structural VAR model for the UK economy (Brignone & Piffer) — with no replication package. This project re-implements the full pipeline in Python — data assembly from public sources, Bayesian estimation, identification, and every output figure — and validates it against the paper's published results.

Within the suite it is the empirical, time-series member: it imposes just enough structure (zero and sign restrictions) to name the shocks hitting the economy as of the latest ingested data (currently 2024 Q2).

02 — the model in brief

Eight variables, six named shocks.

A VAR in eight quarterly variables — three global, five UK — estimated over 1992Q1–2023Q2 (the inflation-targeting era), in levels with Covid dummies for 2020Q1–2021Q2. The global block uses UK-trade-weighted world aggregates over the euro area, US, Japan, and China.

blockvariables
Global World GDP · World CPI (both UK-trade-weighted) · real oil price in sterling
UK Bank Rate · sterling exchange-rate index · CPI · CPI energy · real GDP

Estimation is Bayesian with a Minnesota (normal-inverse-Wishart) prior plus sum-of-coefficients and dummy-initial-observation priors. Identification follows Arias–Rubio-Ramírez–Waggoner (2018): zero restrictions make the UK a small open economy (UK shocks cannot move world variables on impact) and sign restrictions on impact name six structural shocks — world demand, world energy, world supply, UK demand, UK supply, and UK monetary policy — with importance weights and the Chan–Matthes–Yu (2025) permutation search making the draws efficient. Two unidentified shocks absorb residual volatility.

This is accept/reject sampling: the run behind the results below drew 10,000 posterior (Π, Σ) draws, of which 751 passed the sign restrictions, each importance-weighted — an effective sample size of ~350. Medians and bands are therefore correct but noisier than the paper's 10,000 accepted draws.

03 — quickstart

Estimate, identify, decompose.

The package installs straight from GitHub, with the prepared quarterly dataset inside — boe_var.data.load_data() works immediately. Every input series is public: ONS (CPI, CPI energy, real GDP), Bank of England (Bank Rate, sterling ERI), and FRED (oil, exchange rates, world components). The Bank's internal trade-weighted world GDP and CPI are not published, so they are rebuilt as chain-weighted US + euro-area + Japan + China aggregates with time-varying UK trade weights. 

pip install git+https://github.com/PolicyEngine/boe-var-model

The runner scripts are for development — clone the repository if you want to rebuild the dataset or regenerate the paper outputs: 

python scripts/download_data.py     # reassemble the public data

# the paper's core outputs: IRFs, FEVDs, shocks, historical decompositions
python scripts/run_replication.py

# the forecasting toolkit: fan charts, latest-quarter shock distributions,
# composite IRFs, and the forecast-revision decomposition
python scripts/run_forecast_revision.py

outputs land in results/ — the replication figures (fig2–fig6) plus the forecast figures (fig1, fig7–fig9) and two summary reports.

04 — what it answers

What's happening now, and why.

The SVAR's outputs are diagnoses and forecasts, not reform scores. Four tools, all posterior distributions rather than point estimates:

toolwhat it tells you
IRFs & FEVDs How each named shock propagates through the UK economy, and how much of GDP and inflation variance each explains at every horizon.
Historical decompositions Which shocks drove each episode — how much of the 2022 inflation surge was world energy versus UK demand.
Fan-chart forecasts Unconditional forecasts with credible bands, plus the probability that each shock hitting the latest quarter was positive or negative.
Forecast-revision decomposition The paper's Section 5 exercise: why did the forecast change since last quarter? Splits the revision into news (new shocks) and reassessment, via composite impulse responses — an adding-up identity verified to ~1e-12.

In the shipped 2024Q2 forecast round (6,000 posterior draws, 487 accepted), the tool reads the latest quarter as most likely hit by a positive world-demand shock (probability 0.81) and a negative world-energy shock (0.80), with a composite effect peaking at −0.22 pp on year-on-year CPI inflation and +0.40 pp on GDP growth; the Figure-9 adding-up identity holds per draw to a maximum absolute error of 5.3e-12.

The SVAR does not score reforms — and it won't: mapping a policy reform into identified structural innovations is conceptually dubious, not just unbuilt. Its role is the baseline/conditioning member — it establishes what state the economy is in and how uncertain the baseline is before you simulate changing it with the structural members.
05 — validation

Checked against the published paper.

Validation is against the paper's published figures and headline numbers: Figures 2–6 replicate, and the headline variance decompositions land on the paper's benchmarks. Three independent reviews backed this up — an econometrics review against reference toolboxes (bsvarSIGNs, BEAR, VAR-Toolbox), which caught and fixed a genuine bias in the zero-restriction importance weights; a data-construction review; and a panel-by-panel figure-fidelity review (Figures 3 and 5 pass, 2 and 4 pass with caveats, Figure 6 carries a presentation caveat).

Known caveats: the Bank's internal UK-trade-weighted world aggregates are proxied from public sources; the lag length is assumed (p = 4); the pandemic prior is simplified to exogenous Covid dummies; data are current vintages rather than the paper's August-2024 ones; and the paper's ranking of UK monetary policy as the largest domestic contributor to CPI variance does not replicate (here UK supply, 15.6%, exceeds monetary policy, 7.4%). Every deviation is enumerated in docs/methodology.md.
