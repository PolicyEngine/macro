02 — obr-macro

Against the OBR's own forecast, and HMRC's reckoner.

The emulator is anchored to the November 2025 EFO using the OBR's own add-factor device, so reproducing the published aggregates is a by-construction invariant, not a forecast success: real GDP tracks to a mean absolute percentage error of 0.18% and consumption to 0.29%, against a CI gate of 1%. The number that carries evidential weight is the reform costing below.

The independent check is the reform costing. Raising the basic rate of income tax by 1pp from April 2026 scores at £6.46bn in 2026–27 through the PolicyEngine static-costing bridge, against HMRC's ready-reckoner figure of £6.9bn — a deviation of −6.4%, inside the £6–8bn range spanned by recent published vintages. The gap widens in later years (−15.6% by 2028–29) where HMRC's figures embed administrative-data fiscal drag that survey microdata capture less fully.

The honest scorecard, the two dead equations, outturns, and vintage drift

The honest scorecard. The last row is the one that matters for anyone tempted to read the 0.18% as forecasting skill. The same equations that track the EFO to 0.18% when anchored miss it by 5.70% free-running — precisely the gap the OBR's own add-factor judgement closes in the official process, and the reason reform deltas are always scored against the anchored baseline rather than the raw one. The free-running score is de-seeded, with passthrough variables excluded, and is published report-only. Across the full scorecard only 4 of the 11 computed headline variables land within band (real GDP, consumption, the trade balance, and the trivial employment identity); the remaining 21 headline lines include 10 passthroughs held at the OBR value. The worst line is company profits at 54.57% MAPE, which traces to a single unpublished constant in households' operating surplus OSHH — the paper documents and regression-gates it rather than re-tuning it, since tuning it would be fitting to the answer.

Two equations do not fire. The October 2025 model file contains two household-income equations with a bare log(X) left-hand side — log(HHTFA) and log(NDIVHH). The transpiler now parses that form, but both still fail to execute for an independent reason: their exogenous right-hand-side inputs MAJGDP and CORP are absent from the databank entirely, so the right-hand side evaluates non-finite and the solver skips the update. Every OBR figure on this page is computed with those two channels inert. The practical effect is bounded and demonstrable: household dividend income does not respond to a corporate-profits change, so the household-income channel of a corporation-tax reform is muted — a 5pp corporation-tax rise moves FYCPR by −£1,780m while ΔNDIVHH is exactly zero. Reviving the channel needs a sourced CORP series, a calibration decision rather than a code fix.

Forecast versus outturn. Comparing one forecast vintage with another tests agreement, not accuracy. Against ONS outturns published since anchoring, quarter-on-quarter real GDP growth ran 0.1% in 2025Q2, 0.2% in Q3, 0.2% in Q4 and 0.6% in 2026Q1. The emulator's path (0.15, 0.14, 0.25, 0.37) tracks the three 2025 quarters to within 0.06 percentage points, but — like the November EFO it inherits (0.28, 0.20, 0.27, 0.39) — misses the strong 2026Q1 outturn by roughly a quarter of a point. Two caveats govern the reading: this is primarily a test of the OBR's November vintage, the emulator's own contribution being the 0.02–0.13 point gap between the two model rows; and ONS quarterly estimates are themselves revised, so the outturn is a moving target.

Vintage drift: the March 2026 EFO. Everything above compares against the November 2025 EFO because that is the vintage the emulator is anchored to. It has not been re-anchored to the OBR's subsequent March 2026 forecast, which revises 2026 real GDP growth down to 1.1% — 0.3 points below November — and 2026 CPI inflation down 0.2 points. The anchored baseline's 2026 paths (1.5% GDP growth, 1.4% consumption growth, against the November EFO's 1.4% and 1.2%) therefore now sit 0.3–0.4 points above the official March view. That gap is vintage drift, not model error: it is almost exactly the OBR's own November-to-March revision, and an emulator re-anchored to the March tables would inherit the new path by construction. The lesson is about levels, not deltas — reform effects are differences between structurally identical runs and are insensitive to modest baseline drift, but anyone quoting the anchored baseline should treat it as a November 2025 snapshot the official forecaster has since marked down.

03 — boe-svar

Against a Bank of England paper, and against outturns.

The replication target is Brignone & Piffer (2025), Bank of England Macro Technical Paper No. 3. Its headline calibration figure is the share of UK forecast-error variance attributed to identified global shocks — world demand, energy, and supply — at the one-year horizon: roughly 40% for GDP and roughly 50% for CPI. The full 10,000-draw production run yields 40.9% and 50.1%, deviations of a percentage point or less.

Why the fast CI configuration lands 7–8 points off

The deliberately cheap unweighted CI configuration lands at 47.4% and 42.4% — 7–8 points off, in opposite directions for the two variables. That gap is itself informative rather than embarrassing: the importance weights correct the Arias et al. (2018) zero-restriction sampler towards the uniform-over-rotations posterior, and applying them moves both shares onto the published values. Both configurations sit inside the wide [30, 60]% acceptance band that CI enforces, which is chosen to catch gross regressions without being flaky to Monte-Carlo variation.

Out-of-sample. Forecasting seven quarters ahead from a frozen 2024Q2 data edge, the root-mean-squared error over 2024Q3–2026Q1 is 0.32 percentage points for both GDP growth and CPI inflation, with all fourteen outturns inside the 68% credible bands. The scorecard is mixed in an informative way: activity is tracked well throughout, while the medians progressively undershoot the 2025 inflation re-acceleration — outturns of 3.5% and 3.8% against medians of 3.2%, still inside the band but on its upper half. The model was right that the hump would mean-revert and wrong, by up to 0.6 points, about its peak.

Why there is no official yardstick for those forecast errors

No official counterpart exists for those RMSEs. The authors' companion paper (Staff Working Paper No. 1,165, January 2026) is a methods paper on a different, four-variable SVAR; it publishes no root-mean-squared errors, coverage rates or benchmark comparisons, so the forecast numbers here stand without an official yardstick and are reported as such.

04 — frb-us

Against the Fed's own solver — and against the Fed's own noise floor.

The tracking invariant is the sharpest test in the suite: after init_trac, solving the baseline must reproduce the Fed's LONGBASE database exactly for all 284 endogenous variables in all 20 quarters. The maximum absolute error across all 5,680 variable-quarters is 5.6×10−17 — machine precision, against a CI gate of 10−8.

The shock-response comparison needs a yardstick, and the honest one is the reference implementation's own version-to-version variation. Our responses match the vendored pyfrbus 1.0.0 to 6.0×10−9 and the current 1.1.1 release to 1.4×10−8. But the Board's two releases — whose model.xml and LONGBASE are byte-identical, differing only in the Newton routine's LU-factorisation reuse — differ from each other by 1.3×10−8. Our agreement therefore sits at the scale of the reference implementation's own numerical noise, not at some tolerance we chose. All three residuals concentrate in the same near-zero series (wpsn, zgap05).

Multipliers. Government-purchases and personal-tax shocks of 1% of GDP land inside every published range they are compared against — the last three rows below.

Two scope caveats: expectations path, and the 2014 FEDS Note comparison

Only the VAR-expectations path was exercised in the version comparison; model-consistent expectations are not implemented here, so forward-guidance-style exercises are out of scope. And published 100bp funds-rate responses are read off the 2014 FEDS Note charts, so those comparisons are approximate by nature — our output-gap trough of −0.50pp against the Note's ≈−0.4pp is within vintage and shock-design differences, not an exact match.

05 — pe-microsim

Against statute itself.

The microsimulation is a different kind of object from the four macro members, and it is validated differently. It does not forecast, so it has no forecast error; it encodes UK and US tax and benefit law as parameterised statute and computes every variable for a specific household. The ground truth is the law — checkable rule by rule, line by line, against the legislation and the official guidance. For an exact household, the arithmetic is exact, not an approximation.

That is a strong form of validation for the question it answers ("what does this reform mean for a nurse on £35,000 with two kids?") and it does not transfer to population aggregates, where the binding constraint is the microdata rather than the rules: population-level scoring runs on a 53,000-household enhanced Family Resources Survey for the UK and CPS microdata for the US. The clearest read on how those aggregates land against an official costing is the 1pp basic-rate comparison above, which runs through this engine — a −6.4% deviation from HMRC in the first year, widening later. That residual is attributable to survey-versus-administrative data vintage, baseline policy assumptions, and HMRC's embedded taxable-income elasticities, none of which are rule-encoding errors.

06 — psl-og

No ground truth. Calibration targets instead.

Read this section differently. Unlike the OBR emulator, the structural VAR, and frb-us, the overlapping-generations member has no published ground truth to replicate. What follows is a comparison of the deployed calibration's targets against current official UK aggregates. Where the model matches, that is because the quantity was imposed or targeted — it is anchoring, not validation, and it is labelled as such in every row. This model is a structural counterfactual: you can audit its assumptions and its code, but you cannot check its answer against anyone's published answer, because there isn't one.

The treatment column is the whole point: imposed quantities cannot disagree with the data, so a zero deviation there carries no information.

What would count as over-identifying evidence, and the dating and structural caveats

The genuinely over-identifying checks are the ones the steady state must deliver: the capital–output ratio and the interest rate consistent with the calibrated (γ, δ, β) triple. These emerge in an economically sensible range in the deployed configuration, but no systematic published reconciliation of achieved-versus-target moments exists for OG-UK 0.3.0, and the working paper declines to manufacture one. Producing that reconciliation from logged solves is the single most valuable next step for this member.

Two dating caveats: the official column reports figures current at mid-2026 while the deployed calibration was frozen against slightly earlier vintages, and the saving ratio is volatile quarter to quarter, so β is matched to a smoothed level rather than the latest print. Two structural caveats carried over from OG-Core: the ability types and lifetime earnings profiles are still the US tax-microdata estimates, and UK re-estimation is an open task.
